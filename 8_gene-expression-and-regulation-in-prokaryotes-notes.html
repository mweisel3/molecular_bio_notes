Gene Expression and Regulation in Prokaryotes

▶What is differential gene expression?

Nearly all cells in an organism contain the same DNA, but they can be vastly different in structure and function (e.g., a neuron vs. a skin cell). This is because they express different sets of genes.

  • Gene Expression: The process by which the information in a gene is used to create a functional product, either an RNA or a protein.
  • Housekeeping Genes: Expressed in almost all cells at a constant level because they are required for basic cellular functions.
  • Tissue-Specific Genes: Expressed only in a particular type of cell, giving that cell its specialized function.
▶At what levels can gene expression be controlled?

Gene expression can be regulated at many different steps from DNA to protein. In eukaryotes, these include:

  1. Chromatin Remodeling: Controlling how tightly the DNA is packed.
  2. Transcriptional Control: The main point of regulation; determining which genes are transcribed and how often. This is a key step for regulation since it requires a lot of energy to transcribe.
  3. RNA Processing Control: Controlling how the pre-mRNA is spliced and processed.
  4. Translational Control: Controlling how often an mRNA is translated into protein.
  5. Protein Activity Control: Controlling the activity or degradation of the final protein product. For most genes, transcriptional control is the most important level of regulation.
▶What are the key players in transcriptional regulation?

Regulatory proteins bind to DNA near a gene's promoter to control its transcription. There are two main types:

  • Repressors: These proteins block or reduce transcription. They often work by physically blocking RNA polymerase from binding to the promoter.
  • Activators: These proteins promote or increase transcription. They often work by helping RNA polymerase bind more efficiently to the promoter.
▶What is the lac operon?

The lac operon in E. coli is a classic example of gene regulation. It is an operon, meaning a cluster of genes transcribed together from a single promoter that are involved in a related function—in this case, the metabolism of lactose.

  • Structural Genes:
    • lacZ: Codes for β-galactosidase, the enzyme that breaks lactose down into glucose and galactose.
    • lacY: Codes for lactose permease, a protein that transports lactose into the cell.
    • lacA: Codes for transacetylase.
  • Regulatory DNA Elements:
    • Promoter (P): The binding site for RNA polymerase.
    • Operator (O): The binding site for the repressor protein. It is located downstream of the promoter.
▶How does the lac operon activator (CAP) work?

The lac operon is also controlled by an activator protein called CAP (Catabolite Activator Protein). This system links the operon to the cell's preferred energy source, glucose.

  • Low Glucose: When glucose levels are low, levels of a signaling molecule called cAMP become high.
  • cAMP-CAP Complex: cAMP binds to the CAP protein, activating it.
  • Activation: The active cAMP-CAP complex binds to the CAP site on the DNA (upstream of the promoter). This binding helps recruit RNA polymerase to the promoter, activating transcription.
  • High Glucose: When glucose is present, cAMP levels are low, the CAP protein is inactive, and it does not bind to the DNA.
▶How does the lac operon repressor (LacI) work?

The lac operon is controlled by a repressor protein called LacI (encoded by the lacI gene, which is separate from the operon). This system responds to the presence of lactose.

  • No Lactose: The LacI repressor protein is active and binds tightly to the operator sequence on the DNA. This physically blocks RNA polymerase from moving forward, repressing transcription.
  • Lactose Present: When lactose is available, it is converted into allolactose. Allolactose acts as an inducer by binding to the LacI repressor protein. This changes the repressor's shape, causing it to fall off the operator DNA. With the operator clear, RNA polymerase can now (if helped by CAP) transcribe the operon.
▶How is the lac operon controlled by both glucose and lactose?

The lac operon is only expressed at high levels when its preferred food (glucose) is absent AND its alternative food (lactose) is present. This dual control results in four possible states:

  1. +Glucose, -Lactose: The repressor is bound (blocking), and the activator is off. RESULT: Transcription is OFF.
  2. +Glucose, +Lactose: The repressor is removed, but the activator is still off because cAMP is low. RNA polymerase can bind weakly on its own. RESULT: Very little transcription.
  3. -Glucose, -Lactose: The activator is bound (promoting), but the repressor is also bound (blocking). The repressor wins. RESULT: Transcription is OFF.
  4. -Glucose, +Lactose: The repressor is removed, AND the activator is bound. RNA polymerase is strongly recruited to the promoter. RESULT: Abundant transcription.
▶How can you measure the level of gene expression?

There are several ways to detect and quantify the products of gene expression:

  • RNA Level:
    • Northern Blot: A technique to detect a specific RNA sequence from a mixture. RNA is separated by size on a gel, transferred to a membrane, and then a labeled DNA/RNA probe is used to detect the RNA of interest.
    • RT-PCR (Reverse Transcription PCR): A very sensitive method to detect and quantify RNA. RNA is first converted into DNA (cDNA) using reverse transcriptase, and then the cDNA is amplified using PCR.
  • Protein Level:
    • Western Blot: Detects a specific protein using antibodies.
    • Bioassay: A test that measures the biological activity of the protein (e.g., an enzyme assay).
▶What are constitutive mutants?

A constitutive mutant is a mutant strain in which a gene that is normally inducible (can be turned on and off) is expressed continuously, regardless of the presence of an inducer.

  • Discovery: Jacob and Monod found rare blue colonies of E. coli on a plate with X-Gal but without the inducer (IPTG).
  • Conclusion: These bacteria had a mutation that caused the lac operon to be "stuck" in the ON position. Such mutations could be in the gene for the repressor protein (lacI) or in the repressor's binding site on the DNA (the operator).
▶How can you distinguish between a repressor mutation and an operator mutation?

To figure out where the constitutive mutation was, scientists created merozygotes—bacteria that have their own chromosome plus an F-factor plasmid carrying a second copy of the lac operon. This allows testing how different versions of the genes and sites interact.

  • Hypothesis 1 (Mutant Repressor): If the repressor gene (I⁻) is broken, it makes a non-functional repressor. Introducing a plasmid with a functional repressor gene (I⁺) will provide a working repressor protein that can act on both the chromosomal and plasmid operators. This restores normal, inducible control.
  • Hypothesis 2 (Mutant Operator): If the operator sequence (Oᶜ) is mutated, the repressor protein cannot bind to it, even if the repressor is functional.
▶What is the difference between cis-acting and trans-acting elements?

The merozygote experiments revealed two fundamental types of regulatory elements:

  • Trans-acting factor: A diffusible product, usually a protein (like the LacI repressor), that can move through the cytoplasm and act on any target DNA sequence in the cell (both on the chromosome and on a plasmid). A mutation in a trans-acting factor can be "rescued" by a functional copy elsewhere in the cell.
  • Cis-acting element: A segment of DNA (like the operator or promoter) that only affects the expression of the genes located on the same piece of DNA. A mutation in a cis-acting element (like Oᶜ) cannot be rescued by a functional copy on a different DNA molecule.
▶How can you detect protein-DNA binding?

EMSA is a technique used to detect if a protein binds to a specific DNA fragment.

  • Principle: A DNA fragment will move through a gel at a certain speed. If a protein is bound to it, the DNA-protein complex is larger and bulkier, and will therefore move much more slowly through the gel.
  • Method: A labeled DNA probe is incubated with a protein extract and then run on a gel. A "shifted" band indicates that a protein has bound to the DNA.
  • Specificity: To confirm the binding is specific, a "competition" experiment is done by adding a large excess of the same unlabeled DNA fragment, which should cause the shifted band to disappear.
▶How do you identify the specific DNA sequence a protein binds to?

This technique identifies the exact binding site of a protein on a piece of DNA.

  • Principle: A region of DNA that is bound by a protein is protected from being cut by a nuclease (like DNase I).
  • Method: A DNA fragment is labeled at one end. One sample is incubated with the protein, and a control sample is not. Both are lightly treated with a nuclease that cuts the DNA randomly. When run on a gel, the control lane will show a ladder of bands corresponding to cuts at every position. The sample with the protein will show a similar ladder, but with a gap—the "footprint"—where the bound protein protected the DNA from being cut. This reveals the precise binding sequence.
▶How are reporter genes used in molecular biology?

A reporter gene is a gene whose product (e.g., an enzyme) is easily detectable and is used to study the activity of a regulatory element, like a promoter. The lacZ gene is a common reporter gene.

  • Blue-White Screening: In cloning, a plasmid vector (like pUC18) is often used that contains an ampicillin resistance gene and a lacZ gene. A foreign piece of DNA is inserted into the middle of the lacZ gene, inactivating it.
    • Blue Colonies: Bacteria that get a plasmid with no insert will have a functional lacZ gene, produce β-galactosidase, and turn blue on an X-Gal plate.
    • White Colonies: Bacteria that get a plasmid with the DNA insert will have a disrupted lacZ gene and will be unable to produce functional β-galactosidase, so they remain white. This allows for easy visual identification of successful clones.
▶How is the tryptophan (trp) operon regulated?

The trp operon contains the genes for synthesizing the amino acid tryptophan. Unlike the lac operon, which is inducible (turned on by a substance), the trp operon is repressible (turned off by a substance). It uses two levels of control.

  • Level 1: Repressor Control (On/Off Switch)
    • Tryptophan Absent: The TrpR repressor protein is inactive and cannot bind to the operator. RNA polymerase is free to transcribe the operon, and the cell produces tryptophan.
    • Tryptophan Present: Tryptophan acts as a co-repressor. It binds to and activates the TrpR protein. The active repressor then binds to the trp operator and blocks transcription. This saves the cell energy by not making an amino acid it already has.
▶What is attenuation in the trp operon?

Attenuation is a second, more nuanced level of control that fine-tunes the operon's expression based on tryptophan levels. It relies on a leader peptide sequence in the mRNA that contains two tryptophan codons.

  • High Tryptophan: A ribosome translates the leader peptide quickly without stalling. This allows a terminator hairpin (loop between regions 3 and 4) to form in the mRNA, which stops transcription early.
  • Low/Medium Tryptophan: The ribosome stalls at the tryptophan codons because it's waiting for a tRNA-Trp. This stalling allows an anti-terminator hairpin (loop between regions 2 and 3) to form instead. This structure does not stop transcription, so the polymerase continues and transcribes the rest of the operon's genes at a high level.
▶What are the general models of gene regulation?

There are four basic ways a signaling molecule (ligand) can control a regulatory protein:

  • Negative Regulation (Repressors):
    1. An inducer ligand binds to a repressor, causing it to fall off the DNA, turning the gene ON (like the lac operon).
    2. A co-repressor ligand binds to a repressor, causing it to bind to the DNA, turning the gene OFF (like the trp operon).
  • Positive Regulation (Activators):
    1. An inducer ligand binds to an activator, causing it to fall off the DNA, turning the gene OFF.
    2. An inducer ligand binds to an activator, causing it to bind to the DNA, turning the gene ON (like the CAP protein in the lac operon).
▶What are the main uses of the lac operon system in molecular biology?

The components of the lac operon have been adapted into powerful tools for molecular biology with three main uses:

  1. Inducible Gene Expression: Using the promoter/operator as a molecular "on/off" switch.
  2. Reporter Gene for Cloning: Using the lacZ gene for blue-white screening.
  3. Reporter Gene for Expression Patterns: Using lacZ to see where and when a gene is expressed in an organism.
▶How is the lac system used for inducible gene expression?

Scientists can create plasmids where a gene of interest (e.g., the gene for the human protein G-CSF) is placed under the control of the lac promoter and operator.

  • OFF State: In the absence of an inducer, the LacI repressor (usually supplied by the bacterial host) binds to the operator and prevents the expression of the target gene.
  • ON State: When the synthetic inducer IPTG is added, it removes the repressor, and the cell begins to transcribe and translate the gene of interest, producing a large amount of the desired protein. This allows for controlled, high-level protein production in bacteria.
▶How is lacZ used as a reporter gene in eukaryotes?

To study when and where a specific eukaryotic gene ("your favorite gene" or "yfg") is expressed, scientists can create a transgenic organism (like a mouse).

  • They fuse the regulatory sequences (promoter/enhancer) of "yfg" to the coding sequence of the lacZ gene.
  • Wherever "yfg" would normally be turned on during development, its regulatory sequences will now drive the expression of the lacZ gene instead.
  • By staining the organism or tissue with X-Gal, a blue color will appear in the exact cells and tissues where the gene of interest is active, revealing its expression pattern.
▶What is a Southern Blot and what is it used for?

A Southern blot is a technique used to detect a specific DNA sequence within a complex mixture of DNA (like an entire genome). It allows you to confirm the presence and size of a particular gene or fragment.

▶What are the steps of a Southern Blot?
  1. Digestion: The total genomic DNA is cut into fragments using one or more restriction enzymes.
  2. Gel Electrophoresis: The DNA fragments are separated by size on an agarose gel. This results in a smear of thousands of different fragments.
  3. Transfer (Blotting): The DNA is transferred from the fragile gel to a solid membrane (e.g., nitrocellulose), preserving the exact pattern of the fragments.
  4. Hybridization: The membrane is incubated with a probe—a short, single-stranded piece of DNA that is complementary to the target sequence and has been labeled (e.g., with radioactivity). The probe will only bind (hybridize) to the specific fragment on the membrane that contains the gene of interest.
  5. Detection: The membrane is exposed to X-ray film. A dark band will appear only where the radioactive probe has bound, revealing the size and location of the specific DNA fragment.

Summary

Gene expression is the process of converting genetic information into a functional product and is highly regulated to create different cell types. The main control point is transcription initiation. In prokaryotes, this is controlled by activator and repressor proteins that bind to DNA. The lac operon is a classic example, controlled by two signals: the LacI repressor, which is inhibited by lactose, and the CAP activator, which is stimulated by low glucose. The operon is only fully expressed when lactose is present and glucose is absent, allowing the bacterium to efficiently manage its energy resources.

The tryptophan (trp) operon provides another model of prokaryotic gene regulation, using both a repressor (on/off) and a fine-tuning mechanism called attenuation. The components of the lac operon have been repurposed as versatile tools in molecular biology for inducible protein expression and as a reporter gene in both cloning (blue-white screening) and studying expression patterns in whole organisms. Finally, the Southern blot is a key laboratory technique used to detect a specific DNA sequence from a complex mixture by separating DNA fragments on a gel, transferring them to a membrane, and using a labeled probe to identify the fragment of interest.
