Prokaryotes Translation

▶How are genes organized and transcribed in prokaryotes?

In prokaryotes, genes with related functions are often grouped together in a single unit called an operon.

  • The entire operon is transcribed from a single promoter into one long messenger RNA (mRNA) molecule.
  • This single mRNA, which contains the coding information for multiple proteins, is called polycistronic mRNA.
  • This is in contrast to eukaryotes, where each gene typically has its own promoter and produces a monocistronic mRNA (coding for only one protein).
▶What is coupled transcription-translation?

Because prokaryotes have no nucleus, transcription and translation are not separated in space or time.

  • As the RNA polymerase moves along the DNA transcribing an mRNA molecule, ribosomes can immediately bind to the 5' end of the nascent mRNA and begin translating it into protein.
  • This simultaneous process is known as coupled transcription-translation and allows for very rapid protein synthesis in response to the cell's needs.
▶What are the key molecules involved in translation?

Translation is the process of protein synthesis and requires several key components:

  • mRNA (messenger RNA): Carries the genetic code from the DNA, dictating the amino acid sequence of the protein.
  • Ribosome: A large complex of ribosomal RNA (rRNA) and proteins that acts as the "factory" for protein synthesis. It has a large and a small subunit.
  • tRNA (transfer RNA): Acts as the "adaptor" molecule. It reads the three-letter codons on the mRNA via its anticodon and carries the corresponding amino acid.
  • Amino Acids: The building blocks of proteins.
▶How does the ribosome know where to start translation in prokaryotes?

In prokaryotes, the ribosome identifies the correct start site on the mRNA using two key signals:

  1. The Shine-Dalgarno Sequence: A purine-rich sequence (consensus: AGGAGG) located in the 5' untranslated region (UTR), just upstream of the start codon.
  2. The Start Codon: Almost always AUG, which codes for methionine (Met).
▶What is the genetic code?

The genetic code is the set of rules by which the information in the nucleotide sequence of an mRNA is translated into the amino acid sequence of a protein.

  • Codon: A sequence of three consecutive nucleotides that specifies a particular amino acid.
  • Reading Frame: The code is read in a non-overlapping, continuous sequence of three, starting from the start codon.
  • Degeneracy: The code is redundant, meaning most amino acids are specified by more than one codon. There are 64 possible codons, but only 20 common amino acids.
  • Start/Stop: AUG is the universal start codon (specifying methionine), and three codons (UAA, UAG, UGA) are stop codons that do not code for an amino acid but signal the termination of translation.
▶What are the main steps of translation elongation?

After initiation, the ribosome moves along the mRNA, adding amino acids one by one. The ribosome has three sites for tRNA:

  • A-site (Aminoacyl): The "arrival" site, where a new tRNA carrying its amino acid first binds to the mRNA codon.
  • P-site (Peptidyl): The "polypeptide" site, which holds the tRNA attached to the growing polypeptide chain.
  • E-site (Exit): The "exit" site, where the now-uncharged tRNA is released from the ribosome.
▶How does translation stop?

Translation stops when the ribosome encounters one of the three stop codons (UAA, UAG, or UGA) in the A-site.

  • There are no tRNAs that recognize these codons.
  • Instead, proteins called release factors bind to the A-site.
  • This binding triggers the hydrolysis of the bond linking the polypeptide chain to the tRNA in the P-site, releasing the completed protein.
  • The ribosome then dissociates from the mRNA and separates into its large and small subunits, ready for another round of translation.
▶What is the "wobble" hypothesis?

While there are 61 codons that specify amino acids, most organisms have fewer than 61 different tRNAs. This is possible because of the "wobble" in the third position of the codon.

  • The base pairing between the first two nucleotides of the mRNA codon and the tRNA anticodon is very strict (follows standard Watson-Crick rules).
  • However, the base pairing at the third position of the codon is less critical and can be "wobbly." A single tRNA anticodon can often recognize multiple codons that differ only in their third nucleotide.
  • For example, a G in the "wobble" position of the anticodon can pair with either a C or a U in the third position of the mRNA codon. This flexibility reduces the number of tRNAs needed to read the entire genetic code.
▶How can a specific protein be identified from a mixture?

A Western blot is a technique used to detect and identify a specific protein from a complex mixture of proteins extracted from cells.

  1. Denaturation: Proteins are treated with heat and a detergent (SDS) to unfold them and give them a uniform negative charge.
  2. Gel Electrophoresis (SDS-PAGE): The protein mixture is separated by size by running it through a porous polyacrylamide gel. Smaller proteins move faster and further down the gel.
  3. Transfer (Blotting): The separated proteins are transferred from the gel to a solid membrane (e.g., nitrocellulose).
  4. Detection: The membrane is incubated with a primary antibody that specifically binds to the target protein. A secondary antibody, which is linked to a detection enzyme, is then added. This second antibody binds to the first one.
  5. Visualization: A substrate is added that reacts with the enzyme on the secondary antibody, producing a colored or luminescent signal, revealing a band on the membrane only where the target protein is located.

Summary

In prokaryotes, genes are often grouped into operons, which are transcribed into a single polycistronic mRNA. Because there is no nucleus, translation is coupled with transcription. The process is carried out by ribosomes, which initiate translation by recognizing a Shine-Dalgarno sequence on the mRNA. tRNA molecules act as adaptors, reading mRNA codons and bringing the correct amino acids. The genetic code is degenerate, and wobble pairing allows a single tRNA to recognize multiple codons. Translation proceeds through initiation, elongation, and termination at a stop codon, after which the protein is released. A specific protein can be identified from a cellular extract using a Western blot.
