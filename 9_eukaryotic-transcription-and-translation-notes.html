Eukaryotic Transcription and Translation

▶How do prokaryotic and eukaryotic genes and polymerases differ?
  • Prokaryotic Genes: Are often organized in operons (multiple genes transcribed from one promoter). The coding region is continuous.
  • Eukaryotic Genes: Each gene typically has its own promoter and regulatory regions. The coding sequence is interrupted by non-coding regions called introns, which separate the coding parts, called exons.
  • Eukaryotic RNA Polymerases: Eukaryotes have three different RNA polymerases, each responsible for transcribing different types of genes:
    • RNA Polymerase I: Transcribes most rRNA genes.
    • RNA Polymerase II: Transcribes all protein-coding genes (mRNAs) and some small RNAs.
    • RNA Polymerase III: Transcribes tRNA genes and the 5S rRNA gene.
▶How are transcription and translation separated in eukaryotes?

Unlike prokaryotes where transcription and translation are coupled, in eukaryotes they are separated.

  • Location: Transcription occurs inside the nucleus, while translation occurs in the cytoplasm.
  • Timing: The initial RNA transcript, called pre-mRNA, must undergo several processing steps inside the nucleus before it is mature and can be exported to the cytoplasm for translation.
▶What machinery is required for eukaryotic transcription initiation?

Eukaryotic RNA Polymerase II cannot initiate transcription on its own. It requires a set of proteins called General Transcription Factors (GTFs) to locate the promoter and assemble on the DNA. Key GTFs include TFIIA, TFIIB, TFIID, TFIIE, TFIIF, and TFIIH.

▶What are the core promoter elements in eukaryotes?

The core promoter is the minimal set of DNA sequences required for transcription initiation. It includes several potential elements located around the transcription start site (+1):

  • TATA box: A key sequence located at about -25 to -30.
  • BRE (TFIIB Recognition Element): Located just upstream of the TATA box.
  • Inr (Initiator Element): Surrounds the transcription start site at +1.
  • DPE (Downstream Promoter Element): Located downstream of the start site, at about +28 to +32. Not all promoters have all of these elements; the specific combination influences the promoter's strength and regulation.
▶What is the role of the TATA box and TBP?

The TATA box is a primary recognition site on many promoters.

  • It is bound by the TATA-Binding Protein (TBP).
  • TBP is a subunit of a larger general transcription factor called TFIID.
  • The binding of TBP to the TATA box causes a sharp bend in the DNA, creating a physical landmark that helps attract the other transcription factors and RNA polymerase to the promoter.
▶What are the steps for assembling the preinitiation complex?
  1. TFIID binds: The TBP subunit of TFIID binds to the TATA box.
  2. TFIIA and TFIIB bind: These factors bind next, stabilizing the TFIID-DNA complex and creating a recognition site for RNA Polymerase II.
  3. Polymerase and other factors bind: RNA Polymerase II, along with TFIIF, binds to the promoter.
  4. TFIIE and TFIIH bind: These final factors join the complex. TFIIH is a crucial multi-functional enzyme.
▶How does transcription elongation begin?

For transcription to move from initiation to elongation, the polymerase must break away from the promoter complex. This is accomplished by TFIIH, which has two key enzymatic activities:

  1. Helicase activity: It unwinds the DNA at the start site, creating a transcription bubble.
  2. Kinase activity: It adds phosphate groups to the C-terminal domain (CTD) tail of RNA Polymerase II. This phosphorylation acts as a switch, causing the polymerase to release from the promoter and most of the GTFs, allowing it to begin synthesizing the RNA chain.
▶What are the three major steps of eukaryotic mRNA processing?

Before an mRNA can be exported from the nucleus, it must undergo three processing steps. The enzymes for these steps are recruited by the phosphorylated tail of RNA Polymerase II.

  1. 5' Capping: A modified guanine nucleotide is added to the 5' end of the nascent RNA. This protects the mRNA from degradation and is important for ribosome recognition.
  2. Splicing: Introns are removed and exons are joined together.
  3. 3' Polyadenylation: The 3' end of the mRNA is cleaved at a specific site, and a long chain of adenine nucleotides, the poly-A tail, is added. This increases the stability of the mRNA.
▶How is eukaryotic translation initiated?

Eukaryotic initiation is different from prokaryotic and does not use a Shine-Dalgarno sequence.

  1. The initiator tRNA (carrying methionine) and several eukaryotic initiation factors (eIFs) bind to the small ribosomal subunit (40S).
  2. This complex recognizes and binds to the 5' cap of the mRNA.
  3. The complex then scans down the mRNA in the 5' to 3' direction, searching for the first AUG start codon.
  4. Once the AUG is found, the large ribosomal subunit (60S) joins, the initiation factors are released, and translation begins.
▶What is the Kozak sequence?

The efficiency of finding the first AUG start codon is improved by the presence of a Kozak sequence surrounding it.

  • This is a consensus sequence (e.g., GCCACCAUGG) that is recognized by the scanning ribosome.
  • An AUG codon within a "strong" Kozak sequence is more likely to be selected as the start site, leading to more efficient translation. An AUG in a "weak" context may sometimes be skipped.
▶What is a polyribosome?

A single mRNA molecule can be translated simultaneously by multiple ribosomes.

  • Once the first ribosome has initiated and moved down the mRNA, a new ribosome can bind to the 5' cap and begin translation.
  • This results in a structure called a polyribosome or polysome, which looks like beads on a string, with many ribosomes translating the same mRNA at once.
  • In eukaryotes, the mRNA often forms a loop, with the 5' cap and 3' poly-A tail brought close together, which further increases the efficiency of re-initiation.

Summary

In eukaryotes, transcription and translation are separated by the nuclear membrane. Eukaryotic genes contain introns and are transcribed by one of three RNA Polymerases. RNA Polymerase II transcribes protein-coding genes and requires General Transcription Factors (GTFs) to bind to core promoter elements like the TATA box. After transcription, the pre-mRNA undergoes three key processing steps: 5' capping, splicing, and 3' polyadenylation to become a mature mRNA. Translation is then initiated in the cytoplasm when the ribosome recognizes the 5' cap, scans for the first AUG (often within a Kozak sequence), and begins protein synthesis.
