Prokaryote Transcription

▶What is transcription?

Transcription is the process of creating a complementary RNA copy of a segment of DNA. It is the first major step in gene expression, transferring the genetic information from the stable DNA molecule to a more mobile RNA molecule. Along with replication and repair, it is one of the key processes that requires the DNA double helix to be opened.

▶What are the properties of RNA Polymerase?

RNA Polymerase is the main enzyme that carries out transcription.

  • Similarities to DNA Polymerase:
    • It synthesizes a new nucleic acid strand in the 5' to 3' direction.
    • It requires a DNA template to copy.
    • It adds nucleotides based on complementary base pairing (A pairs with U, G pairs with C).
  • Key Differences from DNA Polymerase:
    • It synthesizes RNA, not DNA, using ribonucleotides (NTPs) instead of dNTPs.
    • It does not require a primer to start a new chain.
    • It lacks 3'→5' exonuclease (proofreading) activity, making it less accurate (error rate of about 1 in 10,000). This is acceptable because RNA is a temporary molecule.
▶What are the three main stages of transcription?
  1. Initiation: RNA polymerase binds to a specific DNA sequence called a promoter, unwinds the DNA to form a transcription bubble, and begins synthesizing RNA.
  2. Elongation: The polymerase moves along the DNA template strand ("downstream"), unwinding the DNA ahead of it and synthesizing the growing RNA chain in the 5' to 3' direction.
  3. Termination: The polymerase reaches a terminator sequence in the DNA, which signals it to stop transcription, release the completed RNA transcript, and detach from the DNA.
▶What is a bacterial promoter?

A promoter is the DNA sequence that signals the start of a gene and is the binding site for RNA polymerase. In bacteria like E. coli, the promoter has two key short sequences, or "consensus sequences":

  • -35 region: A sequence centered approximately 35 base pairs "upstream" (before) the transcription start site. The consensus sequence is TTGACA.
  • -10 region (Pribnow box): A sequence centered approximately 10 base pairs upstream. The consensus sequence is TATAAT. The strength of a promoter (how often it initiates transcription) depends on how closely its -35 and -10 regions match these consensus sequences.
▶How does the promoter determine which DNA strand is the template?

The promoter is asymmetrical, which means it can only be bound by RNA polymerase in one orientation. This orientation determines:

  1. Which of the two DNA strands will be used as the template strand (the one that is "read" by the polymerase).
  2. The direction of transcription.
▶What is the role of the sigma (σ) factor?

Bacterial RNA polymerase is a multi-subunit enzyme. The core enzyme can synthesize RNA but cannot recognize a promoter on its own.

  • The sigma (σ) factor is a detachable subunit that binds to the core enzyme and is responsible for recognizing and binding to the -35 and -10 promoter sequences.
  • Once the polymerase has synthesized the first ~10 nucleotides of RNA, the sigma factor is released, and the core enzyme continues with elongation.
  • After the polymerase terminates, the core enzyme re-associates with a sigma factor to begin the process again on another gene.
▶How does transcription stop in prokaryotes?

There are two main mechanisms for transcription termination in bacteria:

  1. Rho-Independent (Intrinsic) Termination: This is the most common method. The terminator sequence in the DNA codes for an RNA sequence that is self-complementary, allowing it to fold into a stable hairpin loop. This hairpin is followed by a string of Uracil (U) nucleotides in the RNA. The hairpin structure stalls the polymerase, and the weak A-U base pairs holding the RNA to the DNA template are not strong enough to keep it attached, causing the transcript to be released.
  2. Rho-Dependent Termination: This method requires a protein called the Rho factor. Rho is a helicase that binds to the growing RNA transcript and moves along it towards the polymerase. When the polymerase stalls at a specific termination site (often a hairpin, but without the U-rich tract), Rho catches up and uses its helicase activity to unwind the RNA-DNA hybrid, releasing the RNA transcript.
▶How does transcription compare to DNA replication?
  • Scope: Replication copies the entire genome once per cell cycle. Transcription copies only specific parts (genes), and the timing and frequency vary greatly depending on the gene and the cell's needs.
  • Product: Replication produces a double-stranded DNA molecule that remains attached to the template. Transcription produces a single-stranded RNA molecule that is released from the template.
  • Enzymes: Replication uses DNA Polymerase, which requires a primer and has proofreading. Transcription uses RNA Polymerase, which does not require a primer and lacks proofreading.
  • Substrates: Replication uses dNTPs. Transcription uses NTPs.

Summary

Transcription is the 5' to 3' synthesis of an RNA molecule from a DNA template by RNA Polymerase. In prokaryotes, a sigma (σ) factor guides the polymerase to a promoter (containing -35 and -10 consensus sequences), which determines the start site and template strand. After initiation, the σ factor detaches, and the core enzyme elongates the RNA chain. Termination occurs either through the formation of a stable RNA hairpin loop (Rho-independent) or with the help of a Rho factor helicase (Rho-dependent). Unlike DNA replication, transcription is selective, primer-independent, and lacks a proofreading mechanism.
