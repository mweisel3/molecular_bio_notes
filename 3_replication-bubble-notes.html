Replication Bubble

▶What are the key components at a replication fork?

The replication fork is the site of active DNA synthesis, featuring a complex of enzymes working together.

  • Parental DNA Helix: The original double-stranded DNA being copied.
  • Leading Strand: The new DNA strand that is synthesized continuously.
  • Lagging Strand: The new DNA strand that is synthesized in discontinuous fragments.
  • DNA Polymerase: Synthesizes the new DNA strands. Separate polymerase complexes are on the leading and lagging strands.
  • Helicase: Unwinds the parental DNA helix.
  • Primase: Synthesizes RNA primers to start synthesis.
  • Sliding Clamp: Holds the DNA polymerase onto the DNA.
  • Single-Strand Binding Proteins (SSBs): Stabilize the unwound single strands of DNA.
▶How does replication start from an origin?

Replication begins at a specific site called the origin of replication (ori).

  1. Initiator proteins bind to the ori sequence.
  2. This recruits DNA helicase, which unwinds the DNA, and SSB proteins, which keep the strands apart.
  3. DNA Primase synthesizes RNA primers on the exposed single strands.
  4. This process creates a replication bubble with two replication forks that move in opposite directions, initiating bidirectional replication.
▶How do prokaryotes and eukaryotes handle origins of replication differently?

The strategy for initiating replication differs based on chromosome structure and size.

  • Prokaryotes, with a small, circular chromosome, typically have a single origin of replication. Two forks proceed in opposite directions until they meet.
  • Eukaryotes, with large, linear chromosomes, have multiple origins of replication. This allows for the entire chromosome to be copied in a timely manner. Replication bubbles form at each origin and eventually merge.
▶What defines the bacterial origin of replication?

In bacteria like E. coli, the single origin of replication, called OriC, is a specific DNA sequence. It's approximately 240 base pairs long and contains two key features:

  • A-T rich regions: Stretches of DNA rich in Adenine and Thymine, which are easier to unwind because they are held together by only two hydrogen bonds.
  • Binding sites: Specific sequences where the initiator proteins bind to begin the process.
▶How are the leading and lagging strands defined within the replication bubble?

Because replication is bidirectional and DNA polymerase only works 5' to 3', each replication fork has its own leading and lagging strand.

  • On one side of the bubble, the top strand is the template for the leading strand, and the bottom is the template for the lagging strand.
  • On the other side of the bubble, this is reversed. The bottom strand is the template for the leading strand, and the top is the template for the lagging strand. This ensures that overall, both parental strands are replicated.
▶How is DNA replication so fast, and what problems does this create?

Bacterial replication is incredibly efficient, with DNA Pol III synthesizing 1000 nucleotides per second. With two forks, the entire E. coli genome (4.6 million base pairs) can be copied in about 40 minutes.

  • This high speed creates a "winding problem." As the helicase unwinds the DNA at the fork, the DNA ahead of it becomes overwound and tangled, creating supercoils.
  • This torsional stress would eventually stop replication if not resolved.
▶⭐How do cells solve the DNA tangling problem?

Topoisomerases are enzymes that relieve the supercoiling and tangling of DNA. They work by temporarily cutting the DNA backbone, allowing it to relax, and then resealing the cut.

  • Topoisomerase I: Makes a transient single-strand break (a "nick"). This allows the DNA to rotate freely around the intact strand, relieving torsional stress.
  • Topoisomerase II: Makes a transient double-strand break. It can pass one intact double helix through the break in another, which is essential for untangling two intertwined DNA circles after replication.
▶How is replication terminated in bacteria?

In circular chromosomes like in E. coli, replication terminates in a specific region opposite the origin.

  • Ter sites: These are termination DNA sequences.
  • Tus protein (Terminus utilization substance): This protein binds to the Ter sites.
  • The Tus-Ter complex acts as a one-way trap. It allows a replication fork to pass from one direction but blocks it from the other.
  • There are two sets of Tus-Ter sites oriented in opposite directions, creating a "trap zone" where the two replication forks will meet and stop, ensuring termination.
▶How do bacteria ensure replication only happens once per cell cycle?

Bacteria use a timing mechanism based on DNA methylation to prevent re-initiation of replication.

  1. Ready State: Before replication, the origin (specifically GATC sequences within it) is fully methylated on both strands. This is the "go" signal.
  2. Blocked State: Immediately after replication, the DNA is hemimethylated (the old strand is methylated, the new one is not).
  3. Inhibition: A protein called SeqA binds specifically to hemimethylated origins and physically blocks initiator proteins from binding again. This prevents a new round of replication from starting.
  4. Reset: After a delay, the Dam methylase enzyme methylates the new strand. Once the origin is fully methylated again, SeqA detaches, and the origin is reset for the next cell cycle.
▶What are the key differences between prokaryotic and eukaryotic replication?
  • Chromosomes: One circular chromosome in prokaryotes vs. many linear chromosomes in eukaryotes.
  • Origins (Ori): One ori in prokaryotes vs. multiple oris in eukaryotes.
  • Termination: Tus-Ter system in prokaryotes vs. telomeres at the ends of linear chromosomes in eukaryotes.
  • Speed: Prokaryotic replication is much faster (1000 nucleotides/sec) than eukaryotic (50 nucleotides/sec).
  • DNA Packaging: Eukaryotes must also replicate DNA packaged in chromatin (with histones), adding a layer of complexity.
▶What are telomeres and why are they important?

Telomeres are specialized structures at the ends of linear eukaryotic chromosomes.

  • They consist of thousands of short, repeating DNA sequences (in humans, TTAGGG).
  • They have a characteristic G-rich 3' overhang.
  • Function: They act like protective caps, preventing the cell's repair machinery from recognizing the chromosome ends as accidental DNA breaks and fusing them together.
▶How does the cell solve the "end replication problem"?

Linear chromosomes face a problem: during replication of the lagging strand, the final RNA primer at the very end cannot be replaced with DNA, because there is no 3'-OH group for DNA polymerase to add to. This would cause chromosomes to shorten with every cell division.

  • The solution is an enzyme called Telomerase.
  • Telomerase is a ribonucleoprotein (made of RNA and protein) that acts as a reverse transcriptase.
  • It carries its own RNA template, which is complementary to the telomere repeat sequence.
  • It binds to the 3' overhang of the chromosome end and extends it by adding more telomere repeats.
  • This extension provides enough template for primase and DNA polymerase to complete the synthesis of the lagging strand, preventing the chromosome from shortening.
▶How are telomeres protected and regulated?

Telomeres are bound by a complex of protective proteins (including TRF1, TRF2, and POT1), which are sometimes collectively called shelterin.

  • These proteins regulate the length of the telomere by controlling telomerase access.
  • They also help form a structure called a T-loop, where the single-stranded 3' overhang tucks back into the double-stranded region. This "hides" the chromosome end, protecting it from degradation and recognition as a DNA break.
▶What is the Hayflick limit and its connection to telomeres?

Most normal human somatic (body) cells have very low levels of telomerase activity.

  • The Hayflick Limit: The observation that normal human cells in culture can only divide a limited number of times (around 50-60) before they stop, a state called replicative senescence.
  • The Divisional Clock: The prevailing hypothesis is that telomeres act as a "divisional clock." With each cell division, the telomeres get progressively shorter.
  • When they reach a critically short length, a DNA damage signal is triggered, causing the cell to permanently exit the cell cycle. This is thought to be a protective mechanism against cancer.
  • Stem cells and cancer cells often have high telomerase activity, allowing them to overcome this limit and divide indefinitely.

Summary

DNA replication begins at origins, creating a replication bubble with two forks. To manage the tangling caused by rapid unwinding, topoisomerases cut and reseal the DNA. In bacteria, replication ends at Tus-Ter sites and is regulated by methylation, ensuring only one round per cell cycle. Eukaryotes, with linear chromosomes, face an end replication problem, which is solved by the enzyme telomerase that maintains the protective telomere caps. The gradual shortening of telomeres in most somatic cells leads to a finite number of cell divisions known as the Hayflick limit.
