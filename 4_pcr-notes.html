PCR

▶What is PCR and what is its main principle?

PCR is a laboratory method used to amplify (make many copies of) a specific segment of DNA from a larger mixture, like a whole genome. The main principle is to run many cycles of DNA synthesis using specific primers that bracket the target DNA region. This requires prior knowledge of the DNA sequence in the target area to design the primers.

▶What are the three steps of a PCR cycle?

Each PCR cycle consists of three steps distinguished by temperature:

  1. Denaturation (Step 1): The reaction is heated to 94-97°C. This breaks the hydrogen bonds holding the double-stranded DNA, separating it into two single strands to act as templates.
  2. Annealing (Step 2): The temperature is lowered to 55-60°C. This allows the short, single-stranded DNA primers to bind (anneal) to their complementary sequences on the template DNA.
  3. Extension/Synthesis (Step 3): The temperature is raised to 72°C. This is the optimal temperature for Taq DNA polymerase, which binds to the primers and synthesizes a new complementary strand of DNA, extending from the primer.
▶How does the amount of DNA increase during PCR?

The amount of the target DNA increases exponentially with each cycle.

  • First Cycle: Produces 2 double-stranded DNA molecules.
  • Second Cycle: Produces 4 double-stranded DNA molecules.
  • Third Cycle: Produces 8 double-stranded DNA molecules.
  • After 30 cycles, a single starting molecule can be amplified to over 1 billion molecules (2³⁰).
▶What are the key components and temperature settings for PCR?

A standard PCR is performed in an automated machine called a thermocycler and includes:

  • dsDNA Template: The DNA containing the target sequence.
  • Primers: A forward and reverse primer.
  • dNTPs: The four deoxyribonucleoside triphosphates (building blocks).
  • Taq DNA Polymerase: The heat-stable enzyme.
  • The reaction cycles through the three key temperatures: ~95°C (Denaturation), ~55-60°C (Annealing), and 72°C (Extension).
▶Why is a heat-stable polymerase like Taq Polymerase necessary?

PCR requires a special DNA polymerase that can withstand the high temperatures of the denaturation step.

  • Taq Polymerase is the enzyme used, isolated from the bacterium Thermus aquaticus, which lives in hot springs.
  • This enzyme is thermostable, meaning it survives the 95°C denaturation step and has an optimal working temperature of 72°C.
  • Using a heat-stable polymerase allows the entire process to be automated without having to add new enzyme each cycle.
▶How is PCR used to clone a specific gene?

PCR can be used to isolate and amplify a specific gene from genomic DNA.

  1. The DNA sequence of the target region must be known.
  2. Two primers (a "forward" and a "reverse" primer) are designed to bind to the sequences flanking the gene of interest.
  3. Genomic DNA is extracted from cells.
  4. The primers, genomic DNA, Taq polymerase, dNTPs, and buffer are mixed.
  5. The mixture is placed in a thermocycler which automatically performs many cycles of synthesis, resulting in a large quantity of the specific gene.
▶How is primer length determined for PCR?

The length of a primer determines its specificity—how likely it is to appear randomly in a genome.

  • The probability of a specific sequence of length 'n' appearing is (1/4)ⁿ.
  • A 15-base pair sequence has a 1 in 1 billion chance of appearing randomly. In the human genome (~3 billion base pairs), it could appear about 3 times by chance.
  • To ensure a primer binds to a unique location, a length of 18-20 nucleotides is generally specific enough for most known genomes.
▶What is an example of a practical PCR application?

PCR is extremely sensitive, making it ideal for detecting tiny amounts of DNA from pathogens in environmental samples.

  • Example: Detecting the intestinal parasite Cryptosporidium parvum in river water.
  • Water samples are collected, DNA is extracted, and PCR is performed using primers specific to the parasite's DNA.
  • The resulting PCR products are run on a gel. The presence of a band of the correct size (e.g., 522 bp) indicates that the parasite was present in the water sample.
▶What are the main advantages and disadvantages of PCR compared to traditional cloning?
  • Advantages of PCR:
    • Speed: Takes only a few hours.
    • Sensitivity: Can start from a single molecule of DNA.
  • Disadvantages of PCR:
    • Sequence Knowledge: Requires prior knowledge of the target DNA sequence to design primers.
    • Error Rate: Taq polymerase lacks a proofreading mechanism, leading to a higher mutation rate.
    • Size Limitation: The length of the product is typically limited to around 5kb.
    • Contamination Risk: High sensitivity increases the risk of amplifying contaminating DNA.

Summary

PCR (Polymerase Chain Reaction) is a rapid and highly sensitive technique for exponentially amplifying a specific DNA segment. It works by cycling through three temperature-controlled steps: denaturation (95°C), primer annealing (55-60°C), and extension (72°C). The process relies on a heat-stable Taq DNA polymerase and sequence-specific primers to define the target region. While faster and more sensitive than traditional cloning, it requires prior sequence knowledge and can have a higher error rate.
