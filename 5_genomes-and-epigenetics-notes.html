Genomes and Epigenetics

▶Why is DNA packaging necessary?

Every human cell contains approximately 2 meters of DNA that must fit inside a nucleus with a diameter of only a few micrometers. This is a feat of extreme compression, equivalent to packing 24 miles of fine thread into a tennis ball. This is accomplished by packaging the DNA with proteins to form chromatin.

▶What is the basic unit of chromatin?

The first and most basic level of DNA packaging involves proteins called histones.

  • Histone Octamer: The core is made of eight histone proteins (two each of H2A, H2B, H3, and H4).
  • Nucleosome: This is the basic unit, consisting of the histone octamer with 147 base pairs of DNA wrapped around it approximately 1.65 times.
  • This structure shortens the DNA's length by about a third and results in a "beads-on-a-string" appearance, where the nucleosomes are the "beads" and the DNA between them is the "string" (linker DNA).
▶How is chromatin further compacted?

The "beads-on-a-string" fiber is further condensed into more complex structures.

  • 30-nm Fiber (Solenoid): The H1 histone binds to the linker DNA and helps pull the nucleosomes together, twisting them into a helical fiber about 30 nm wide.
  • Looped Domains: The 30-nm fiber is then organized into a series of loops that are anchored to a central protein scaffold. This allows specific regions of the chromosome to be decondensed for gene expression without unpacking the entire chromosome.
  • Mitotic Chromosome: This represents the final, most condensed level of packaging, visible during cell division.
▶How is the genome organized?
  • Genome: The complete set of genetic material in an organism.
  • Chromosome: A single, long DNA molecule that carries a specific set of genes. Humans have 46 chromosomes (23 pairs).
  • Gene: The fundamental physical and functional unit of heredity; a specific segment of DNA that provides instructions for a protein or functional RNA molecule.
  • Organisms can be haploid (one copy of each chromosome, like bacteria) or diploid (two copies of each chromosome, like humans).
  • There is no clear correlation between genome size or chromosome number and the complexity of an organism.
▶What is the human genome composed of?

The human genome is surprisingly complex, with only a tiny fraction coding for proteins.

  • Exons (1.5%): These are the regions of genes that actually code for proteins or functional RNAs.
  • Introns (24%): These are non-coding sequences within genes. They are transcribed into pre-mRNA but are later removed in a process called splicing.
  • Repetitive DNA (~50%): A large portion of the genome is made of various types of repeating sequences, including transposable elements and simple sequence repeats.
  • Unique Non-coding DNA (15%): Other non-coding regions, including regulatory sequences that control gene expression.
  • As organism complexity increases, the proportion of non-coding DNA generally increases as well.
▶What are the two main states of chromatin?

Chromatin in the interphase nucleus exists in two primary states:

  • Euchromatin: A less condensed, "open" form of chromatin. It is rich in genes and is transcriptionally active. It replicates early in the S phase.
  • Heterochromatin: A highly condensed, "closed" form of chromatin. It is gene-poor and transcriptionally inactive. It replicates late in the S phase. Common examples include centromeres and telomeres.
▶What is epigenetics and the "histone code"?

Epigenetics refers to heritable changes in gene expression that do not involve a change in the underlying DNA sequence itself. One of the primary mechanisms of epigenetics is the "histone code."

  • The N-terminal tails of the core histone proteins (H2A, H2B, H3, H4) protrude from the nucleosome.
  • These tails can be chemically modified by enzymes in the cell. The specific pattern of these modifications acts as a "code" that determines the state of the chromatin and how genes are expressed.
▶How do histone modifications work?

Specific chemical groups can be added to or removed from the histone tails, changing chromatin structure. The main types are:

  • Acetylation: The addition of an acetyl group (Ac), typically to lysine residues. This neutralizes the positive charge of the lysine, weakening its interaction with the negatively charged DNA. It is generally associated with euchromatin (open, active genes).
  • Methylation: The addition of a methyl group (Me), also typically to lysines. The effect depends on the specific lysine being modified. For example, methylation of Histone H3 at lysine 9 (H3K9me) is a classic mark of heterochromatin (closed, silent genes).
  • Phosphorylation: The addition of a phosphate group (P). The balance between enzymes that add these marks (e.g., HATs - histone acetyltransferases) and those that remove them (e.g., HDACs - histone deacetylases) controls the local chromatin state.
▶What is DNA methylation?

In addition to histone modifications, DNA itself can be methylated. This is another key epigenetic mechanism.

  • In mammals, a methyl group (CH₃) is added to the 5th carbon of the cytosine base.
  • This typically occurs where a cytosine is followed by a guanine, known as a CpG island.
  • DNA methylation is strongly associated with gene silencing and the formation of heterochromatin.

Summary

To fit ~2 meters of DNA into the nucleus, it is compacted into chromatin. The basic unit is the nucleosome (DNA wrapped around a histone octamer), which is further coiled into 30-nm fibers and organized into loops. The human genome consists mostly of non-coding DNA, including introns and repetitive elements. Chromatin exists in two states: open euchromatin (active genes) and closed heterochromatin (silent genes). The switch between these states is controlled by epigenetic mechanisms, primarily the histone code (covalent modifications like acetylation and methylation on histone tails) and DNA methylation, which regulate gene expression without altering the DNA sequence.
